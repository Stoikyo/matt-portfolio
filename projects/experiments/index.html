Why experiments

I use experiments to test ideas cheaply, learn new tools and see what’s worth turning into a full product. Most of these live in the overlap between AI, automation and simple utilities that make decisions easier or workflows lighter.

AI Photo Fit Checker

  • Prototype: snap an automotive accessory and get a quick fit read for your vehicle.
  • Built an upload → analyse → respond flow with image recognition plus prompt logic.
  • Goal was behavioural signal, not perfect accuracy: does “take a photo to check fit” feel natural?
  • Outcome: people preferred the photo-first flow over manual search, pointing to a “scan-to-check” pattern.

Novated lease tools in Firebase

  • Modelled simple novated lease scenarios: repayments, pre-/post-tax, and comparisons.
  • Built in Firebase to keep experiments fast, interactive and low-friction.
  • Focused on clarity over complexity so the numbers felt understandable.
  • Learnings fed into how I explain EV costs and decision flows elsewhere.

Motoru & EVPicker automations

  • Used Google Apps Script to glue forms, sheets and email: matching quiz answers to EVs, routing leads, sending structured emails.
  • Logged results for analysis to tune matching logic without heavy backend work.
  • Turned basic forms/spreadsheets into product-like behaviour while iterating fast.

Jaan — Small business workflow setup

  • Helped a carpenter start his business by untangling quoting/admin stuck in notebooks and basic accounting tools.
  • Moved to a simple, repeatable Xero workflow with quote/invoice templates and material tracking.
  • Mapped the journey from first enquiry to final invoice, comparing quoted vs actual work.
  • Focused on “boring done well”: clear steps, fewer surprises, and a system that supports the work.

Closing note

Together, these experiments are where I try new ideas, kill weak ones quickly and sharpen how I think about systems. They’re not polished products, but they’ve had a big influence on the tools and experiences I’ve gone on to build — and there’s more to come. I’m always tinkering, learning new tools and looking for the next thing worth turning into something real.
