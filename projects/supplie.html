Supplie - AI Decision Support Exploration

Concept prototype · 2026

Exploring how AI could help operational teams make better decisions when dealing with messy supply chain and logistics data.

View site ↗

Background / Problem

Operational teams often rely on dashboards and spreadsheets to track what has already happened. These tools are useful, but they rarely help teams explore what might happen next.

The idea behind Supplie was to explore whether AI models could help surface useful signals from messy operational data and support planning decisions rather than just reporting.

The focus is not automation. The focus is helping humans understand complex systems faster.

Approach

I started by looking at how operational teams actually make decisions. A lot of the work happens in spreadsheets, Slack threads and meetings where people are trying to interpret partial information.

From there I explored a few directions:

  • identifying signals that matter in supply chains and logistics
  • experimenting with ways AI models could surface patterns or anomalies
  • prototyping interfaces where teams could explore scenarios rather than just viewing reports
  • exploring simulation-style approaches to planning

Most of the work so far has been early exploration and prototyping rather than a production system.

Outcome / What I learned

A few interesting themes came out of the exploration.

  • Operational data is messy. Real-world systems rarely have clean inputs.
  • AI is most useful when it helps people reason about decisions rather than when it tries to replace the decision entirely.
  • The most interesting opportunity may be helping teams explore scenarios rather than generating answers.

System Concept

Supplie explores how AI could help organisations make better operational decisions by combining multiple signals and translating them into actionable insights.

The idea is to move beyond simple dashboards or forecasts and instead build a reasoning layer that helps interpret complex data.

Conceptually the system works in three stages:

Signals → Reasoning → Decisions

Signals Layer

The first layer aggregates operational signals that influence decisions.

Examples include:

  • demand signals
  • inventory levels
  • lead times
  • supply disruptions
  • external events such as weather or logistics constraints

These signals form the raw inputs for the system.

Reasoning Layer

The reasoning layer uses AI to interpret how these signals interact.

Instead of simply forecasting demand, the goal is to identify patterns such as:

  • emerging supply risks
  • unexpected demand spikes
  • regional imbalances
  • downstream operational impacts

The reasoning layer attempts to connect signals across time and location to highlight meaningful changes in the system.

Decision Layer

The final layer translates those insights into potential actions.

Examples might include:

  • adjusting supply allocations
  • flagging emerging shortages
  • suggesting logistics adjustments
  • identifying where intervention may be required

Rather than replacing planners or operators, the system is intended to act as a decision-support tool.

Prototypes & Experiments

Early work on Supplie has focused on exploring how AI could interpret complex operational signals.

Experiments have included:

  • testing signal aggregation models
  • exploring how AI could reason about changing supply conditions
  • prototyping decision-support workflows

These experiments have helped shape the architecture and identify where AI may provide meaningful assistance.

Next Technical Steps

Future development would focus on building a more complete system architecture.

This would likely include:

  • a structured signal ingestion pipeline
  • graph-based data models for representing supply networks
  • AI reasoning systems capable of analysing signal interactions
  • simulation and forecasting models
  • interfaces for operational teams to explore recommendations

The long-term goal is to move from passive reporting systems to AI-assisted operational decision making.
